# Pattern: Size a Market I'm Considering Entering

> "I want to size a market I'm considering entering"

**Principles used**: Chain-of-Thought Scaffolding, Specificity Amplification, Negative Examples

---

## When to Use
- Evaluating whether a market opportunity is worth pursuing
- Need TAM/SAM/SOM for a pitch deck or internal planning
- Validating (or invalidating) a market hypothesis

## The Pattern

```
Size the market for {{PRODUCT/SERVICE}} targeting {{CUSTOMER_SEGMENT}}.

My product: {{ONE_SENTENCE_DESCRIPTION}}
Price point: {{PRICE}} per {{UNIT — seat/month/transaction/etc.}}
Geography: {{GEOGRAPHIC_SCOPE}}

Work through this with explicit math:
1. TAM (Total Addressable Market): How many potential customers exist in this geography? What's the maximum revenue if 100% adopted? Show the multiplication.
2. SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market): Of the TAM, what portion can we actually reach given our distribution channels, language, and product limitations?
3. SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market): Realistically, what market share can a new entrant capture in years 1-3? Benchmark against comparable companies at similar stages.

For each number:
- Show the assumptions (population size, conversion rate, price)
- Rate the assumption quality: (backed by data | reasonable estimate | pure guess)
- Show what the number becomes if assumptions are 2x optimistic

Do NOT: Use "the global X market is $Y billion" from analyst reports as your TAM. Build bottom-up from actual customer counts.
```

## Why It Works
Requiring bottom-up math prevents the classic trap of top-down TAM ("1% of a $100B market = $1B!"). Rating assumption quality forces intellectual honesty. The 2x sensitivity check reveals which assumptions actually matter.

## Common Mistakes
- Using top-down TAM from analyst reports → wildly inflated, not actionable
- Not specifying geographic scope → TAM includes markets you can't serve
- Forgetting to benchmark SOM against comparable startups → unrealistic capture rates
