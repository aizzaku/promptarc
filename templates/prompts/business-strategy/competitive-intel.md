# Pattern: Analyze My Competitive Landscape

> "I want to analyze my competitive landscape"

**Principles used**: Specificity Amplification, Chain-of-Thought Scaffolding, Constraint-Driven Creativity

---

## When to Use
- Understanding who you're competing against (including indirect competitors)
- Finding gaps in the market that competitors aren't addressing
- Positioning your product against existing alternatives

## The Pattern

```
Analyze the competitive landscape for {{MY_PRODUCT}}: {{ONE_LINE_DESCRIPTION}}.

My target customer: {{SPECIFIC_CUSTOMER_PROFILE}}
My price point: {{PRICE_RANGE}}

Competitors I know about: {{LIST_KNOWN_COMPETITORS}}

For each competitor (and any major ones I've missed):
1. What they do (one sentence — not their marketing pitch, the actual product)
2. Who they serve best (their ideal customer vs. mine)
3. Pricing model and range
4. Their main strength (the one thing they do better than anyone)
5. Their main weakness (the one thing their customers complain about)
6. Threat level to me: High / Medium / Low — with reasoning

Also analyze:
- The status quo (what do people do WITHOUT any of these products? This is your biggest competitor.)
- Indirect substitutes (different products solving the same underlying problem)

Conclude with: Where is the gap? What customer need is underserved by ALL current options?

Do NOT: Copy competitor marketing copy. I need honest assessment, not their self-description.
```

## Why It Works
Including the status quo as a competitor addresses the most common blind spot in competitive analysis. Asking for the honest assessment rather than marketing copy prevents just regurgitating competitor websites.

## Common Mistakes
- Only analyzing direct competitors → misses substitutes and the status quo
- Using competitor self-descriptions → biased toward their strengths
- No threat-level assessment → everything looks equally concerning
