# Pattern: Research a Topic for Content

> "I want to research a topic for an article I'm writing"

**Principles used**: Context Layering, Specificity Amplification, Chain-of-Thought Scaffolding

---

## When to Use
- You have an angle and need supporting evidence
- Building the factual foundation before writing
- Need to understand what's already been published on this angle

## The Pattern

```
I'm writing about: {{ANGLE — specific thesis, not just topic}}
For: {{AUDIENCE}} on {{PLATFORM}}

I need research on:
1. Data/statistics that support or challenge this thesis
2. Named examples (companies, people, events) that illustrate the point
3. Expert perspectives — who has credibility on this topic and what's their position?
4. Counter-arguments — what's the strongest case against my thesis?

For each finding:
- State the specific fact or claim
- Source it (who says this, when, where)
- Rate your confidence in the source (primary research | reputable secondary | uncertain)
- Note how I'd use it in the piece (opening hook | supporting evidence | counter-argument | closing insight)

Flag anything you're uncertain about with [VERIFY].
```

## Why It Works
Structuring research by purpose (how you'd use it) makes the output immediately actionable for writing. Requiring counter-arguments prevents confirmation bias in the research phase.

## Common Mistakes
- Researching a topic instead of an angle → too broad, not actionable
- Not asking for counter-arguments → piece lacks credibility
- Not requiring source confidence ratings → mixing reliable and unreliable data

## Variations
- **Interview prep**: "I'm interviewing {{PERSON}} about {{TOPIC}}. What are 5 questions that would reveal something new — not the softball questions every interviewer asks?"
- **Competitive content analysis**: "Find the top 5 articles on {{TOPIC}}. For each: what's their angle, what do they miss, where can I add value?"
