# Pattern: Decompose a Problem from First Principles

> "I want to decompose a complex problem from first principles"

**Principles used**: Chain-of-Thought Scaffolding, Specificity Amplification

---

## When to Use
- Conventional approaches aren't working
- You suspect accepted wisdom might be wrong in your context
- Need to understand WHY something works, not just WHAT to do

## The Pattern

```
I want to think about {{PROBLEM}} from first principles.

The conventional wisdom says: {{WHAT_EVERYONE_DOES_OR_BELIEVES}}
I suspect this might be wrong because: {{YOUR_DOUBT}}

Decompose:
1. What are the fundamental truths here? (Not best practices — actual constraints that can't be argued. Physics, math, human nature, economics.)
2. What are the assumptions layered on top? (Things we treat as truth but are actually conventions, habits, or historical artifacts.)
3. For each assumption: Is this still true? Was it ever true, or was it always a shortcut?
4. If we strip away the invalid assumptions, what are the possible approaches? (Don't limit to known solutions — reason from the fundamentals.)
5. Of these approaches, which is best for MY specific situation?

Ground rules:
- "Everyone does it this way" is not a reason. WHY does everyone do it this way?
- "It's industry standard" is not a reason. Standards exist for specific contexts — does that context match mine?
- Contrarian for the sake of being contrarian is as bad as conventional thinking. The goal is truth, not novelty.
```

## Why It Works
Separating fundamental truths from assumptions reveals which "best practices" are actually just habits. Requiring justification for conventions prevents cargo-culting.

## Common Mistakes
- Treating all conventions as wrong → reinventing the wheel on things that are conventional for good reason
- Not stating the fundamental constraints → reasoning in a vacuum
- First principles becoming an excuse to ignore domain expertise → balance fresh thinking with existing knowledge
