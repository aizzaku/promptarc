# Pattern: Stress-Test My Thinking with Opposing Views

> "I want to stress-test my thinking with opposing views"

**Principles used**: Role and Perspective Framing, Constraint-Driven Creativity, Chain-of-Thought Scaffolding

---

## When to Use
- You have a strong opinion and want to check for blind spots
- Making a decision that's hard to reverse
- Preparing for pushback on a recommendation

## The Pattern

```
My position: {{YOUR_ARGUMENT/DECISION/PLAN}}
My reasoning: {{WHY_YOU_BELIEVE_THIS}}

Steelman the opposing view. Argue AGAINST my position as if you genuinely held the opposing view and were trying to convince a neutral third party. No strawmen — find the strongest version of the counter-argument.

Then:
1. Which counter-arguments are legitimate concerns I should address?
2. Which counter-arguments have weaknesses? (Explain what's wrong with them.)
3. Does the opposing view reveal any blind spots in my reasoning?
4. Given the strongest opposing arguments, should I: (a) maintain my position, (b) modify it, or (c) reconsider entirely?

Important:
- Don't hedge. If the opposing view is stronger than my position, say so.
- Don't softball the opposition to make me feel good about my position.
- If my reasoning has a fatal flaw, I need to know now — not after I've committed.
```

## Why It Works
Requiring a genuine steelman (not strawman) forces the strongest possible challenge. The explicit instruction not to hedge overcomes Claude's default agreeableness.

## Common Mistakes
- Weak opposition that's easy to dismiss → confirms bias instead of testing it
- Not asking "should I reconsider?" → gets the exercise but not the actionable conclusion
- Using this as a one-time check instead of iteratively → strongest insights come from multiple rounds
