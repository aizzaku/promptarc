# Pattern: Facilitate a Group Decision

> "I want to facilitate a group decision"

**Principles used**: Context Layering, Chain-of-Thought Scaffolding, Specificity Amplification

---

## When to Use
- Team can't agree on a decision
- Need a structured process to avoid endless debate
- Want to ensure all voices are heard, not just the loudest

## The Pattern

```
I need to facilitate a group decision about: {{DECISION}}

Participants: {{WHO — roles and their likely perspectives}}
Current state: {{WHERE_THE_DISCUSSION_IS — haven't started / debating / stuck / deadlocked}}
Decision authority: {{WHO_MAKES_THE_FINAL_CALL — consensus required / one person decides after input / majority vote}}
Time pressure: {{WHEN_THIS_NEEDS_TO_BE_DECIDED}}

Design a facilitation process:

1. Problem framing: A shared statement of what we're deciding and why, that all participants would agree describes the situation. (Misaligned problem statements = guaranteed disagreement.)
2. Option generation: How to surface ALL options without premature evaluation. (Separate ideation from judgment — critique kills creativity.)
3. Criteria alignment: What factors matter, in what order? Get agreement on CRITERIA before evaluating OPTIONS. (Most group conflicts are actually criteria conflicts in disguise.)
4. Structured evaluation: How each option performs against each criterion. Make it visible (table, scorecard).
5. Dissent capture: Before deciding, ask explicitly: "Who disagrees and why?" Record the dissent. (They might be right later.)
6. Decision + commitment: The decision, the reasoning, and each person's commitment to support it even if they disagreed.

For my specific situation, what's the highest-risk step? Where is this group most likely to get stuck?
```

## Why It Works
Separating criteria alignment from option evaluation prevents the most common group decision failure: arguing about solutions when you actually disagree about what matters. Capturing dissent creates a record that can be revisited if the decision doesn't work out.

## Common Mistakes
- Jumping to solutions before agreeing on criteria → circular debate
- Letting the highest-rank person speak first → anchoring effect kills alternative ideas
- No explicit dissent capture → silent disagreement becomes passive resistance
