# Pattern: Set Goals I'll Actually Follow Through On

> "I want to set goals that I'll actually follow through on"

**Principles used**: Specificity Amplification, Constraint-Driven Creativity, Negative Examples

---

## When to Use
- Setting goals for a new period (quarter, year)
- Current goals feel vague or uninspiring
- History of setting goals and not completing them

## The Pattern

```
I want to set goals for {{TIME_PERIOD}}.

Areas I want to make progress in:
{{LIST — career, health, creative, financial, learning, relationships, etc.}}

My track record with goals (honest): {{USUALLY_COMPLETE | HIT_OR_MISS | RARELY_FOLLOW_THROUGH}}
What typically goes wrong: {{WHY_GOALS_FAIL_FOR_YOU — lose motivation, too ambitious, forget about them, priorities shift}}

For each area, help me set:
1. The outcome goal: What's true at the end of {{TIME_PERIOD}} that isn't true now? (Measurable, with a deadline.)
2. The lead measure: What's the recurring action I control that drives the outcome? (Daily or weekly habit.)
3. The anti-goal: What am I explicitly NOT optimizing for? (Prevents scope creep into adjacent goals.)
4. The minimum viable version: If life gets chaotic, what's the bare-minimum version of this goal that still counts as progress?
5. The failure protocol: When I miss a day/week, what's the restart procedure? (Not "try harder" — an actual procedure.)

Stress-test each goal:
- Is the lead measure something I can do on my worst day?
- Is the outcome measurable enough that there's no ambiguity about whether I hit it?
- Does the minimum viable version prevent all-or-nothing thinking?

Maximum 3-5 goals total. If I listed more areas than that, help me pick which ones matter most for THIS period.
```

## Why It Works
Lead measures focus on actions within your control. Anti-goals prevent unbounded optimization. The failure protocol addresses the #1 killer of goals: the first stumble becoming a permanent quit. Capping at 3-5 prevents dilution.

## Common Mistakes
- Goals that are wishes ("be healthier") instead of measurable outcomes
- No lead measures → outcome-only goals feel out of control
- No failure protocol → first missed day triggers abandon-the-whole-thing
