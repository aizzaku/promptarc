# Pattern: Prioritize When Everything Feels Urgent

> "I want to prioritize when everything feels urgent"

**Principles used**: Chain-of-Thought Scaffolding, Constraint-Driven Creativity, Specificity Amplification

---

## When to Use
- Everything on the list feels important and urgent
- Can't decide what to work on next
- Overwhelm is causing paralysis

## The Pattern

```
I'm overwhelmed. Here's everything on my plate:
{{LIST_ALL_ITEMS — tasks, projects, obligations, requests}}

Help me prioritize. For each item, assess:
1. What happens if I don't do this for 2 weeks? (Nothing? Someone's upset? Revenue lost? Something breaks?)
2. Am I the only person who can do this? (Or could it be delegated, deferred, or dropped?)
3. Does this move my top goal forward? (What's my top goal: {{TOP_GOAL}})

Then categorize:
- DO NOW: Items where delay has real consequences AND only I can do them
- SCHEDULE: Important but not time-sensitive — block specific time for these
- DELEGATE: Someone else can do this (name who, or identify what's blocking delegation)
- DROP: Saying no. Not "maybe later" — actually removing it from the list.

Limit DO NOW to 3 items max. If more than 3 are genuinely urgent, I have a capacity problem, not a prioritization problem — flag that.

For my top 3, give me the order and the reasoning. Start with the one that unblocks the most other items.
```

## Why It Works
The "2-week test" separates real urgency from perceived urgency. Capping DO NOW at 3 forces actual prioritization instead of re-labeling everything as urgent. Flagging capacity problems prevents treating a structural issue as a personal failing.

## Common Mistakes
- Every item marked "urgent" → prioritization exercise that doesn't prioritize
- Not including "drop" as a category → list only grows, never shrinks
- Ignoring capacity constraints → no amount of prioritization fixes overcommitment
