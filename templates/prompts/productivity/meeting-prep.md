# Pattern: Prepare for an Important Meeting

> "I want to prepare for an important meeting"

**Principles used**: Context Layering, Chain-of-Thought Scaffolding, Specificity Amplification

---

## When to Use
- High-stakes meeting (investor, client, leadership)
- Complex meeting with multiple agenda items
- Meeting where you need to drive a specific outcome

## The Pattern

```
Help me prepare for a meeting.

Meeting: {{WHAT_IS_IT — e.g., "Q1 board review" / "client pitch" / "1:1 with manager"}}
Attendees: {{WHO_IS_THERE_AND_THEIR_ROLE}}
Duration: {{LENGTH}}
My role: {{WHAT_IM_EXPECTED_TO_DO — present, decide, listen, persuade}}

My goal: {{WHAT_I_WANT_TO_WALK_OUT_WITH — a decision, a commitment, information, alignment}}

Prepare:
1. Agenda: What should be discussed, in what order, with time allocations? (Lead with the most important item — don't save it for "any other business.")
2. Pre-read: What should attendees know BEFORE the meeting? (Send this in advance to avoid spending meeting time on information transfer.)
3. For each agenda item:
   - The question to be answered or decision to be made
   - The options (if it's a decision)
   - Your recommendation (if you have one)
   - The likely objections and your responses
4. Parking lot: What topics might come up that should NOT be discussed in this meeting? (Have a deflection: "Great point — let's schedule a separate session for that.")

Time check: Does this agenda fit in {{LENGTH}}? If not, what gets cut?
```

## Why It Works
Pre-reads prevent the most common meeting waste: spending meeting time on information that could be read. The objection-response prep prevents being caught off guard. The parking lot prevents scope creep.

## Common Mistakes
- Overstuffed agenda → nothing gets resolved, everything gets touched
- No clear desired outcome → meeting becomes a discussion, not a decision
- No pre-read → first 30% of meeting is context-setting
